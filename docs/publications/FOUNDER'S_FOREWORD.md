# Founder's Foreword

There are moments in every generation when humanity must decide what it considers worth preserving.

Some preserve monuments.

Some preserve works of art.

Some preserve memories.

I chose to help preserve books.

More specifically, I chose to help preserve the words that have shaped civilizations, inspired scholarship, strengthened faith, and connected generations across centuries.

The inspiration for Biblion began long before a single line of code was written.

It began with an admiration for those who devoted their lives to preserving knowledge for people they would never meet.

Among those individuals, Desiderius Erasmus has long stood as an example to me. His commitment to careful scholarship, faithful preservation of texts, and belief that knowledge should be made more accessible continues to inspire the work behind this project.

Biblion is not an attempt to replace that tradition.

It is an attempt to continue it.

The tools have changed.

The responsibility has not.

Today we possess technologies that previous generations could scarcely imagine. High-resolution imaging, digital preservation, machine learning, and artificial intelligence allow a single individual to accomplish work that once required entire teams of specialists.

These technologies should not diminish our appreciation for human scholarship.

They should extend it.

Artificial intelligence is neither the author nor the authority.

It is a tool—one capable of assisting historians, theologians, philosophers, librarians, archivists, translators, and researchers as they preserve humanity's written heritage.

I believe that AI, used responsibly, can help us recover fading texts, improve transcription, organize knowledge, and make historical works more accessible to future generations.

For me, that mission begins with Scripture.

My Christian faith has profoundly shaped both my life and my purpose. I believe the world deserves reliable methods of preserving God's Word and the many historical works that help us understand it more deeply. My hope is that Biblion will encourage others to see artificial intelligence not as a replacement for wisdom, but as a means of extending the reach of faithful scholarship, careful research, and thoughtful stewardship.

Biblion is therefore more than software.

It is an invitation.

An invitation to preserve.

An invitation to study.

An invitation to teach.

An invitation to build.

Whether you approach these texts as a believer, a historian, a philosopher, a linguist, a librarian, a software engineer, or simply as someone who values humanity's written record, I hope you will find something here worth continuing.

This project also represents something deeply personal.

Like many people, I have been reminded that our time is limited. That realization has not discouraged this work—it has clarified it. If Biblion outlives me, then it has accomplished one of its greatest purposes: enabling others to preserve, understand, and share knowledge long into the future.

No meaningful work is ever completed by one person alone.

Every manuscript preserved.

Every page restored.

Every transcription corrected.

Every contribution made by a volunteer, scholar, developer, editor, or reader becomes part of something larger than any individual.

That has always been true.

My sincere hope is that Biblion will become one more link in that long chain of stewardship.

If this project helps preserve even a small part of humanity's written heritage for those who come after us, then it will have fulfilled its purpose.

— Max Richey

Founder, Biblion
